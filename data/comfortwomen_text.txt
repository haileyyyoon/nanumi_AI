Comfort women are women and girls across the Asia-Pacific region who were forcibly recruited, abducted, or deceived into Japan’s military sexual slavery system during WWII. 

Comfort women were confined in military-run brothels called "comfort stations" and subjected to systematic rape and abuse by Japanese soldiers. 

The euphemism comfort women obscures the reality of militarized sexual violence; terms like "sexual slaves" more accurately describe their experiences. Chongsindae (Volunteer Labor Corps) refers to non-sexual labor roles such as munitions factory workers, not to be confused with comfort women. Confusing the two groups distorts historical records and minimizes the unique suffering of each. 

The comfort station system began in the early 1930s after the Manchurian Incident and escalated with the Second Sino-Japanese War in 1937. Reasons for the system included: curbing mass rape, controlling STDs, and maintaining discipline within military ranks. Women were recruited through deceptive job ads, abductions, or were sold due to poverty. 

Many victims were illiterate and unaware of what "comfort women" meant, leading to unknowing conscription. Survivors report that they were not informed of their actual duties and had no means of escape once trapped. 

There were around 200,000 comfort women. This number is only an estimate.

Comfort Women victims included women from Korea, China, Japan, Taiwan, the Philippines, Indonesia, East Timor, Burma, Vietnam, and the Netherlands. 

Around 240 Korean women have publicly testified, admitting they were former comfort women. 

Former comfort women often remained silent due to stigma and trauma until the early 1990s. 

On August 14, 1991, Kim Hak Soon testified publicly, marking the start of global awareness. 

On January 8, 1992, the first Wednesday Demonstration was held by the Korean Council in front of the Japanese Embassy in Seoul. 

In the 1990s onward, the House of Sharing was established and lawsuits were filed against Japan in Tokyo courts. 

The Wednesday Demonstrations have been held weekly since 1992 to demand a formal apology and reparations from Japan. These demonstrations are organized by the Korean Council for Women Drafted for Military Sexual Slavery by Japan. The protests aim to raise awareness, secure justice, and preserve memory. 

Comfort women were forced to serve between 10 to 50 soldiers a day depending on rank and time. 

Comfort stations operated with military-issued currency called scripts that the women could not use. 

Living conditions in comfort stations were unsanitary and washbasins were rarely cleaned, increasing risk of STIs. Women were stripped of their names and given Japanese names displayed on wooden panels. 

Comfort stations varied between permanent structures and mobile military setups. 

The different types of comfort stations are military-run or civilian-run but all operated under military control. 

In 1992, the Japanese military issued a limited apology known as the Kono Statement, which admitted involvement.  However, the Kono Statement was later undermined by conservative efforts to revise history. 

Survivors filed lawsuits, like the one led by Kim Hak Soon, which reached partial success before being overturned. 

United Nations Reports from 1996 and 1998 confirmed the comfort women system as war crimes and demanded compensation and prosecution. 

In 2007, U.S. House Resolution 121 urged Japan to accept responsibility and educate future generations. 

Survivors faced long-term health and psychological consequences. 

Many women from Okinawa, including Koreans and locals, were also forced into sexual slavery. Civilians during the Battle of Okinawa witnessed comfort women hiding in caves or searching for food. 

South Korea began investigating in 1992 and restarted in 2004 with the Truth Commission. The government initially chose not to demand compensation, a decision criticized for overlooking victims' rights. 

A 2006 Constitutional Court ruling confirmed that the government failed to uphold survivors’ rights. Comfort women remain a pressing issue in Korean society, both culturally and institutionally. 

Activists continue to demand formal apology, truth disclosure, memorials, restitution, historical education, and criminal accountability.

The national memorial day for comfort women is on August 14th. This day commemorates the experiences of women forced into sexual slavery by the Japanese military during World War II. It was officially designated as a national memorial day by the South Korean government in 2017. The date was chosen because it marks the first public testimony of a former "comfort woman," Kim Hak-sun, in 1991

Park Du-ri (1923-2006) was born the eldest of seven children in Miryang, Gyeongsangnam-do. At seventeen she was deceived by a promise of factory work in Japan and was instead taken to Taiwan, where she was forced into military sexual slavery for about five years. At the House of Sharing she was known for expressing her pain and loneliness through humor and song, and for making people laugh despite her hardship. She died in February 2006.

Park Ok-ryeon (1919-2011) was born in Muju, Jeollabuk-do. At twenty-three she was taken to Rabaul in Papua New Guinea and forced into military sexual slavery. She twice boarded ships trying to escape the island under bombing, and each time the ship was destroyed by bombing and rough seas; of about 50 women who attempted the crossing, only 4 survived. In 1944 she was finally able to leave by ship through Shimonoseki and returned to Korea in 1945. Though usually quiet, she was respected as a steady, dependable leader among the survivors living at the House of Sharing. She died in May 2011.

Kim Sun-deok (1921-2004) was born in Uiryeong, Gyeongsangnam-do. At seventeen, in 1937, she was deceived by an offer to work as a nurse and was taken to Shanghai, where she was forced into military sexual slavery; she later passed through Nanjing and returned to Korea in 1940 with the help of a Japanese military officer. She painted "The Flower That Could Not Blossom," an image that became a symbol of the survivors' experience. She was known as an energetic, active leader at the House of Sharing and died on the morning of June 30, 2004, the day of a Wednesday Demonstration she had always attended without fail.

Kim Hak-soon (1924-1997) was born in Jilin, China, and raised in Pyongyang after her father's death. At fifteen she was sent to a gisaeng (Korean entertainer) training house, and at seventeen, in 1941, she traveled toward China with her foster father in search of work. In Beijing she was seized by Japanese soldiers and forced into military sexual slavery, an ordeal that included repeated rape from the first night of her captivity. She later escaped with the help of a Korean man posing as a merchant, eventually returning to Korea after the war. On August 14, 1991, she became the first Korean survivor to give public testimony about her experience, an act that is widely credited with launching international awareness of the comfort women issue and that later led South Korea to designate August 14 as its national memorial day for comfort women victims.

Kang Duk-kyung (1929-1997) was born in Jinju, Gyeongsangnam-do. In June 1944, at age sixteen, she was sent to Japan's Fujikoshi aircraft parts factory in Toyama prefecture as a member of the Women's Volunteer Corps, a wartime labor-mobilization program that Japan officially distinguished from the comfort women system but that in her case became a route into it. After enduring hunger, twelve-hour shifts, and harsh conditions at the factory, she attempted to escape and was instead caught by a Japanese soldier and forced into a military comfort station. Confined and made to serve soldiers there, she eventually escaped again with the help of a Korean civilian and returned to Korea, later suffering the death of an infant child and lasting reproductive health problems. In her later years she became known for expressing her experiences through painting.

Kang Il-chul (born 1928) was born in Sangju, Gyeongsangbuk-do, the youngest of seven surviving children in a farming family that struggled with poverty. As a teenager during the early 1940s she was taken to a Japanese military comfort station in China. She is among the survivors who gave extensive oral testimony to researchers, describing her family's background, the circumstances of her recruitment, and daily life inside a comfort station.

A survivor who testified under the alias Kim Deok-jin, born Kim Sun-deok in 1921 in Uiryeong, Gyeongsangnam-do, described how her father died after being beaten by a Japanese police officer, deepening her family's poverty and leading her into domestic service as a child. In 1937, at seventeen, she was recruited under the pretense of factory work, taken by ship through Nagasaki, and forced into a comfort station in Shanghai that later moved with Japanese troops toward Nanjing. She returned to Korea after the war with the help of a Japanese military officer.

Kim Sun-ok (Chinese name Kim Sook-ran), born in 1922 in Pyongyang, was mobilized around 1940 to a Japanese military comfort station in Dongning county, Heilongjiang province, China, where she was given the Japanese name Kayoko. She remained in China after the war, married a Chinese man, and was located and interviewed there by Korean researchers in 1988; by the time of the interview she was reluctant to discuss her past publicly out of concern for her family's feelings.

Park Ok-sun, a survivor who gave testimony in Japan, described being born in Busan and being seized by two men while walking near her home at age fifteen. She rejected the term "comfort women," saying the comfort stations were not fit for human habitation, and called on Japan to formally apologize and provide compensation to survivors before they died. Her testimony is preserved in both Korean and Japanese.

Beyond Korea, women from many countries were victimized by Japan's wartime comfort station system, including China, Taiwan, the Philippines, Indonesia, East Timor, Malaysia, the Netherlands, and Japan itself. After Kim Hak-soon's 1991 testimony, survivors from these countries also came forward despite difficult circumstances, and their testimonies show that the comfort women issue extends beyond Korea-Japan relations to broader questions of colonialism, wartime sexual violence, and women's human rights.

Wan Ai-hua (born 1930, Inner Mongolia, China) was the first Chinese woman to testify publicly as a survivor of wartime sexual violence. In 1943, during a Japanese military campaign against anti-Japanese guerrillas, she was abducted and tortured on three separate occasions because she was a Communist Party member and village leader; one assault broke her pelvis, reducing her height from 165 to 147 centimeters.

Several Taiwanese survivors have testified. Lu Man-mei, deceived at seventeen in 1943 by an offer of restaurant work, was instead taken to Hainan Island and forced into a comfort station; she was permitted to leave only after eight months of pregnancy, and her baby died 38 days after birth. Deng Gao Bao-zhu was taken to Guangdong, China at seventeen in 1938 and later moved through Hong Kong, Singapore, Burma, and Rangoon before returning to Taiwan via Vietnam after Japan's defeat. Ian Apai, an Indigenous Truku woman from Hualien, was recruited for sewing work near a Japanese army post and was instead taken to a cave used as a comfort station, where she was forced to serve soldiers daily until 1946. Hwang Ah-tao was deceived at twenty by a "nurses wanted" advertisement and taken to a comfort station beside an air base in the mountains of Balikpapan, Indonesia.

Maria Rosa Luna Henson (1927-1997) of Luzon, Philippines, was the first Filipina survivor to testify publicly, doing so in September 1992. She was raped by Japanese soldiers at age fourteen, before she had begun menstruating, and later joined anti-Japanese guerrilla activity; in 1943 she was captured and held for nine months of confinement and repeated rape. Other Filipina survivors include Maxima Regala de la Cruz, abducted with her mother near San Ildefonso in Bulacan province, and Tomasa Dioso Salinog, whose father was killed by a Japanese soldier who then abducted her at age thirteen and confined her at a military post until Japan's defeat.

Jan Ruff-O'Herne (born 1923), a Dutch woman living in Japanese-occupied Indonesia, was held at the Ambarawa internment camp after Japan's 1942 invasion of Java and was later confined for two months in a comfort station called "Nanamitei" in Semarang in 1944. She became the first Dutch survivor to testify publicly, in 1992.

Sirota Suzuko (a pseudonym, 1921-1993) was the first Japanese survivor to testify publicly. Sold by her impoverished family at age seventeen, she was taken together with Korean and Okinawan women to comfort stations across the South Pacific. After the war she returned to Japan but struggled with a difficult life and eventually entered a shelter; a memorial to her and other survivors was built in 1986 after her account became publicly known.

Rosalind Saw (born 1916, Penang, Malaysia) was confined in an army comfort station known as the "Donrotku Hotel" in Penang until early 1945 and gave birth to a Japanese soldier's child that February; she disclosed her experience publicly in 1994. Mardiyem (1929-2007) of Yogyakarta, Indonesia, was deceived at age thirteen by a local Japanese-appointed official and forced into a comfort station in Banjarmasin, where she became pregnant and was subjected to a forced abortion before being confined there until Japan's defeat; she later became an important figure in a survivors' network. Esmeralda Boe of East Timor was taken while still too young to have begun menstruating and was forced to serve three Japanese commanders over three years, including being made to sing for their entertainment.

In August 1993, after evidence of the Japanese military's involvement in the comfort station system became public, Chief Cabinet Secretary Kono Yohei issued the Kono Statement, Japan's first official acknowledgment that the military was directly or indirectly involved in establishing and managing comfort stations, and that many women were gathered against their own will through coercion or deception. The Kono Statement remains the Japanese government's official position on the issue, although conservative politicians in Japan have repeatedly called for it to be revised or retracted.

Starting in December 1991, Kim Hak-soon and other survivors filed lawsuits against the Japanese government, and survivors and their supporters from Korea, the Philippines, Korean residents of Japan, the Netherlands, China, Taiwan, and Hainan Island pursued roughly nine separate court cases in Japan. Japanese courts largely dismissed these suits on statute-of-limitations and pre-war state-immunity grounds. The one partial victory, a 1998 Shimonoseki district court ruling that recognized state responsibility for legislative inaction, was later overturned on appeal. Most cases did, however, result in Japanese courts formally acknowledging the historical facts of the victims' suffering as established evidence, even while denying legal remedies.

Coverage of the comfort women system in Japanese history textbooks expanded after the Kono Statement, appearing in all seven major Japanese high school textbook publishers by 1997 and in all middle school history textbooks that same year. A backlash led by the "Japanese Society for History Textbook Reform," founded in 1996, campaigned to remove such references; by 2006, references to comfort women had disappeared from every major Japanese middle school history textbook then in use.

In July 1995 Japan established the Asian Women's Fund, formally the "Asian Peace National Fund for Women," which used privately raised donations to pay "atonement money" to individual survivors together with a letter of apology from the prime minister, while the Japanese government separately funded medical and welfare support; the fund dissolved in March 2007. It reached 285 survivors in Korea, the Philippines, and Taiwan, 79 survivors in the Netherlands, and funded 69 elderly-care welfare facilities in Indonesia. Critics, including most Korean survivor-support organizations, argued that the fund substituted private donations for formal state compensation, reached only a fraction of victims, limited the prime minister's apology letter to only those who accepted the fund's payment, and did not resolve survivors' underlying demand for legal responsibility and apology from the Japanese state.

The comfort women issue was raised at the United Nations Human Rights Commission starting in 1992. UN Special Rapporteur Radhika Coomaraswamy investigated and submitted a report in 1996, and Special Rapporteur Gay McDougall submitted a further report in 1998; both concluded that the wartime comfort station system violated international law and that Japan bore responsibility for compensating victims and prosecuting those responsible. Beginning in 1995, the International Labour Organization's Committee of Experts likewise found in multiple reports that the comfort station system violated the Forced Labour Convention.

In July 2007, the United States House of Representatives passed House Resolution 121, led by Congressman Mike Honda, calling on Japan to formally and unambiguously apologize for and accept historical responsibility for the comfort women system; survivors testified at a February 2007 congressional hearing that helped build support for the resolution despite lobbying against it by the Japanese embassy and a newspaper advertisement placed by conservative Japanese lawmakers. Similar resolutions followed in the parliaments of the Netherlands, Canada, the European Parliament representing the 27 EU member states, the Philippines, Taiwan, and South Korea, as well as in several Japanese city assemblies.

In December 2000, women's organizations from across Asia convened the unofficial Women's International War Crimes Tribunal in Tokyo, intended to address the exclusion of wartime sexual violence from the postwar International Military Tribunal for the Far East (the Tokyo Trials, 1946-1948). Sixty-four survivors from eight countries participated as witnesses, and roughly 5,000 observers from 30 countries attended, including a joint North and South Korean team of prosecutors made possible by the political thaw following the 2000 inter-Korean summit. On December 12, 2000, the tribunal issued a summary judgment finding Japan's wartime Emperor Hirohito and other military and government officials guilty of crimes against humanity; a final judgment followed in The Hague in December 2001. The tribunal had no legal enforcement power, but was widely seen as a significant civil-society effort to establish moral and historical accountability, and its proceedings allowed some former Japanese soldiers to publicly confess their actions.

In 2001, Japan's public broadcaster NHK aired a documentary about the 2000 Women's Tribunal that was later found to have been altered under political pressure from ruling-party politicians seeking to minimize the comfort women issue; investigative reporting in 2005 revealed that Shinzo Abe, then deputy chief cabinet secretary, was among the officials involved in pressuring the broadcaster. A Japanese civic group, VAWW-NET Japan, sued NHK over the alteration; it won at the Tokyo High Court in 2007, but the ruling was overturned by Japan's Supreme Court in 2008.

When the comfort women issue emerged in Korea in January 1992, the South Korean government formed a task force under the Ministry of Foreign Affairs to investigate the historical record and register victims, publishing an interim report that July; a more thorough government investigation did not resume until 2004, under the Truth Commission on Forced Mobilization under Japanese Imperialism. In 1993, President Kim Young-sam announced that Korea would not seek material compensation from Japan in order to occupy the moral high ground, a policy that has remained largely in place since, even as the Korean government has separately provided survivors with domestic financial, medical, and housing support. Critics argue that this "no compensation demand" stance, while intended to keep moral and diplomatic pressure on Japan, has also constrained recognition of survivors' individual legal claims. In 2011, South Korea's Constitutional Court ruled that the government's failure to actively pursue a resolution of survivors' compensation claims through diplomatic channels with Japan was itself unconstitutional.

On January 8, 2021, the Seoul Central District Court ordered the Japanese government to pay 100 million won (roughly 90,000 US dollars at the time) to each of twelve Korean survivors and bereaved families who had sued for damages in the case registered as 2016 Gahap 505092. The court rejected Japan's argument that it was immune from being sued in a foreign country's courts, reasoning that the doctrine of sovereign immunity should not shield acts amounting to crimes against humanity. Japan did not participate in the proceedings and did not recognize the ruling, and it remains unenforced against the Japanese state; the case was nonetheless significant as the first ruling by a domestic court anywhere to hold the Japanese government directly liable to individual comfort women survivors.

A diary kept by a Korean man who worked as a paymaster and manager at Japanese military comfort stations in Burma and Singapore during 1943 and 1944 was discovered in a private museum in Gyeonggi-do, South Korea, and was translated and published by researchers in Seoul and Japan starting in 2013. The diary is considered a rare surviving administrative record of the day-to-day operation of comfort stations from the perspective of station management rather than the victims, and researchers have used it to help verify details about how the comfort station system was organized and run.

The House of Sharing's Museum of Japanese Military Sexual Slavery by Japan was made possible by contributions from many individuals, Buddhist and Catholic religious communities, civic organizations, universities, and companies in Korea and Japan, reflecting broad public support across both countries for preserving survivors' history and testimony.

Among the most widely known 'comfort women' survivor-activists and artists are: Kim Hak-soon (1924-1997), whose public testimony on August 14, 1991 was the first by a Korean survivor and launched the international movement for awareness and redress; Kang Duk-kyung (1929-1997), who became known in her later years for expressing her experiences through painting; Kim Sun-deok (1921-2004), who painted "The Flower That Could Not Blossom," an image that became a symbol of the survivors' experience, and who was an energetic, active leader at the House of Sharing; Park Du-ri (1923-2006), remembered at the House of Sharing for expressing her pain and loneliness through humor and song; and Park Ok-ryeon (1919-2011), respected as a steady, dependable leader among the survivors living at the House of Sharing. Other important voices include Kang Il-chul (born 1928), who gave extensive oral testimony to researchers, and Park Ok-sun, who testified in Japan and rejected the term 'comfort women.' Among survivors from other countries, the first to testify publicly include Maria Rosa Luna Henson of the Philippines (1992), Jan Ruff-O'Herne of the Netherlands (1992), Wan Ai-hua of China, and Sirota Suzuko of Japan.
