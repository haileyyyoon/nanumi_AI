Comfort women are women and girls across the Asia-Pacific region who were forcibly recruited, abducted, or deceived into Japan’s military sexual slavery system during WWII. 

Comfort women were confined in military-run brothels called "comfort stations" and subjected to systematic rape and abuse by Japanese soldiers. 

The euphemism comfort women obscures the reality of militarized sexual violence; terms like "sexual slaves" more accurately describe their experiences. Chongsindae (정신대, Volunteer Labor Corps) refers to non-sexual labor roles such as munitions factory workers, not to be confused with comfort women. Confusing the two groups distorts historical records and minimizes the unique suffering of each. 

The comfort station system began in the early 1930s after the Manchurian Incident and escalated with the Second Sino-Japanese War in 1937. Reasons for the system included: curbing mass rape, controlling STDs, and maintaining discipline within military ranks. Women were recruited through deceptive job ads, abductions, or were sold due to poverty. 

Many victims were illiterate and unaware of what "comfort women" meant, leading to unknowing conscription. Survivors report that they were not informed of their actual duties and had no means of escape once trapped. 

The number of 'comfort women' victims is estimated at between 50,000 and 300,000; around 200,000 is a commonly cited figure. These figures are estimates of the total number of victims across the Asia-Pacific region. Most of the women were in their teens when they were taken. Separately from these overall estimates, in South Korea about 240 women have publicly come forward and testified that they were victims.

Comfort Women victims included women from Korea, China, Japan, Taiwan, the Philippines, Indonesia, East Timor, Burma, Vietnam, and the Netherlands. 

240 Korean women have publicly testified, admitting they were former comfort women. As of July 2026, only five registered survivors remain alive in South Korea, and no halmonies live at the House of Sharing anymore: the last four residents — Lee Ok-sun (two survivors shared this name), Park Ok-sun, and Kang Il-chul — were transferred to hospital care facilities as their health declined, and Kang Il-chul passed away in 2026. 

Former comfort women often remained silent due to stigma and trauma until the early 1990s. 

On August 14, 1991, Kim Hak Soon testified publicly, marking the start of global awareness. 

On January 8, 1992, the first Wednesday Demonstration was held by the Korean Council in front of the Japanese Embassy in Seoul. 

In the 1990s onward, the House of Sharing was established and lawsuits were filed against Japan in Tokyo courts. 

The Wednesday Demonstrations have been held weekly since 1992 to demand a formal apology and reparations from Japan. These demonstrations are organized by the Korean Council for Women Drafted for Military Sexual Slavery by Japan. The protests aim to raise awareness, secure justice, and preserve memory. 

Comfort women were forced to serve on average 10 to 30 soldiers a day, with even higher numbers on weekends — by some accounts up to 50 — depending on rank and time. 

Comfort stations operated with military-issued currency called scripts that the women could not use. 

Living conditions in comfort stations were unsanitary and washbasins were rarely cleaned, increasing risk of STIs. Women were stripped of their names and given Japanese names displayed on wooden panels. 

Comfort stations varied between permanent structures and mobile military setups. 

The different types of comfort stations are military-run or civilian-run but all operated under military control. 

In 1992, the Japanese military issued a limited apology known as the Kono Statement, which admitted involvement.  However, the Kono Statement was later undermined by conservative efforts to revise history. 

Survivors filed lawsuits, like the one led by Kim Hak Soon, which reached partial success before being overturned. 

United Nations Reports from 1996 and 1998 confirmed the comfort women system as war crimes and demanded compensation and prosecution. 

In 2007, U.S. House Resolution 121 urged Japan to accept responsibility and educate future generations. 

Survivors faced long-term health and psychological consequences. 

Many women from Okinawa, including Koreans and locals, were also forced into sexual slavery. Civilians during the Battle of Okinawa witnessed comfort women hiding in caves or searching for food. 

South Korea began investigating in 1992 and restarted in 2004 with the Truth Commission. The government initially chose not to demand compensation, a decision criticized for overlooking victims' rights. 

A 2006 Constitutional Court ruling confirmed that the government failed to uphold survivors’ rights. Comfort women remain a pressing issue in Korean society, both culturally and institutionally. 

Activists continue to demand formal apology, truth disclosure, memorials, restitution, historical education, and criminal accountability.

The national memorial day for comfort women is on August 14th. This day commemorates the experiences of women forced into sexual slavery by the Japanese military during World War II. It was officially designated as a national memorial day by the South Korean government in 2017. The date was chosen because it marks the first public testimony of a former "comfort woman," Kim Hak-sun, in 1991

Park Du-ri (also written Park Doo-ri, 1924-2006) was born in Miryang, Gyeongsangnam-do, the oldest of seven children. In 1940, at the age of sixteen, she was trafficked to Taiwan by a Japanese man who promised her factory work, and suffered five years as a victim of Japanese military sexual slavery, enduring violence so severe it left her with lifelong spinal disease and permanent hearing loss. In 2003 she sued the Japanese government in the Shimonoseki branch of the Yamaguchi district court, demanding an official, unequivocal apology and compensation. At the House of Sharing she was vibrant and full of laughter, gifted in both humor and song, which she used to lessen the pain of her hard life. She passed away on February 19, 2006, at the age of 82.

Park Ok-ryeon (1919-2011) was born in Muju, Jeollabuk-do. In 1941, under the false pretense of recruiting nurses, she was trafficked to the battlefields of Rabaul, Papua New Guinea, where she suffered for three years as a victim of Japanese military sexual slavery. Twice she attempted to escape, but both times the boats were destroyed by air strikes and rough waves and she was forced back to the island; of the roughly 50 women who were brought there, only two survived. She returned to Korea in 1945 and began living at the House of Sharing in 1996, where, though usually quiet, she was respected as a steady, dependable leader among the survivors. In 2007 she recorded a video message urging the U.S. House of Representatives to pass House Resolution 121. She passed away on May 15, 2011, at the age of 92.

Kim Sun-deok (also written Kim Soon-duk, 1921-2004) was born in Uiryeong, Gyeongsangnam-do. In 1937, at the age of sixteen, she was deceived by an offer to work as a nurse and was taken to Shanghai, where she was forced into military sexual slavery; she later passed through Nanjing and was released in 1940 with the help of a Japanese military officer who took pity on her. After witnessing Kim Hak-soon's testimony on television, she decided to speak out and report her status as a victim. She produced many paintings, most famously "Stolen Away" (끌려감) and "Unblossomed Flower" (못다 핀 꽃, also translated as "The Flower That Could Not Blossom"), which became symbols of the survivors' stolen innocence and experience. Sincere and hard-working, she acted as a de facto leader among the victims at the House of Sharing. She suffered a cerebral hemorrhage and passed away on the morning of June 30, 2004 — the day of a Wednesday Demonstration she had always attended without fail. She was 83 years old.

Kim Hak-soon (1924-1997) was born in Jilin, China, and raised in Pyongyang after her father's death. At fifteen she was sent to a gisaeng (Korean entertainer) training house, and at seventeen, in 1941, she traveled toward China with her foster father in search of work. In Beijing she was seized by Japanese soldiers and forced into military sexual slavery, an ordeal that included repeated rape from the first night of her captivity. She later escaped with the help of a Korean man posing as a merchant, eventually returning to Korea after the war. On August 14, 1991, she became the first Korean survivor to give public testimony about her experience, an act that is widely credited with launching international awareness of the comfort women issue and that later led South Korea to designate August 14 as its national memorial day for comfort women victims.

Kang Duk-kyung (also written Kang Duk-kyoung, 1929-1997) was born in Jinju, Gyeongsangnam-do. In 1944, at the age of fifteen, at her Japanese schoolteacher's urging she joined the inaugural all-female labor corps (Chongsindae, 정신대) and was sent to the Fujikoshi aircraft parts factory in Toyama, Japan — a wartime labor-mobilization program officially distinct from the comfort women system, but which in her case became a route into it. After enduring hunger, twelve-hour shifts, and harsh conditions at the factory, she attempted to escape and was captured by the Japanese military police, who forced her into a military comfort station. She eventually escaped again with the help of a Korean civilian and returned to Korea, later suffering the death of an infant child and lasting reproductive health problems. At the House of Sharing she took to painting, leaving behind powerful works such as "Innocence Stolen" (빼앗긴 순정) and "Punish the Guilty! - For the Sake of Peace" (책임자를 처벌하라) depicting the pain she endured. She passed away on February 2, 1997, after a battle with lung cancer, at the age of 68.

Kang Il-chul (1928-2026) was born in Sangju, Gyeongsangbuk-do, the youngest of seven surviving children in a farming family that struggled with poverty. As a teenager during the early 1940s she was taken to a Japanese military comfort station in China. She is among the survivors who gave extensive oral testimony to researchers, describing her family's background, the circumstances of her recruitment, and daily life inside a comfort station. In July 2014, together with fellow survivor Lee Ok-sun, she traveled to Washington, D.C. and met with White House and U.S. State Department officials about the 'comfort women' issue. She lived at the House of Sharing until declining health moved her to a hospital care facility, and she passed away in 2026 as one of the last surviving registered victims.

A survivor who testified under the alias Kim Deok-jin, born Kim Sun-deok in 1921 in Uiryeong, Gyeongsangnam-do, described how her father died after being beaten by a Japanese police officer, deepening her family's poverty and leading her into domestic service as a child. In 1937, at seventeen, she was recruited under the pretense of factory work, taken by ship through Nagasaki, and forced into a comfort station in Shanghai that later moved with Japanese troops toward Nanjing. She returned to Korea after the war with the help of a Japanese military officer.

Kim Sun-ok (Chinese name Kim Sook-ran), born in 1922 in Pyongyang, was mobilized around 1940 to a Japanese military comfort station in Dongning county, Heilongjiang province, China, where she was given the Japanese name Kayoko. She remained in China after the war, married a Chinese man, and was located and interviewed there by Korean researchers in 1988; by the time of the interview she was reluctant to discuss her past publicly out of concern for her family's feelings.

Park Ok-sun, a survivor who gave testimony in Japan, described being born in Busan and being seized by two men while walking near her home at age fifteen. She rejected the term "comfort women," saying the comfort stations were not fit for human habitation, and called on Japan to formally apologize and provide compensation to survivors before they died. Her testimony is preserved in both Korean and Japanese.

Beyond Korea, women from many countries were victimized by Japan's wartime comfort station system, including China, Taiwan, the Philippines, Indonesia, East Timor, Malaysia, the Netherlands, and Japan itself. After Kim Hak-soon's 1991 testimony, survivors from these countries also came forward despite difficult circumstances, and their testimonies show that the comfort women issue extends beyond Korea-Japan relations to broader questions of colonialism, wartime sexual violence, and women's human rights.

Wan Ai-hua (born 1930, Inner Mongolia, China) was the first Chinese woman to testify publicly as a survivor of wartime sexual violence. In 1943, during a Japanese military campaign against anti-Japanese guerrillas, she was abducted and tortured on three separate occasions because she was a Communist Party member and village leader; one assault broke her pelvis, reducing her height from 165 to 147 centimeters.

Several Taiwanese survivors have testified. Lu Man-mei, deceived at seventeen in 1943 by an offer of restaurant work, was instead taken to Hainan Island and forced into a comfort station; she was permitted to leave only after eight months of pregnancy, and her baby died 38 days after birth. Deng Gao Bao-zhu was taken to Guangdong, China at seventeen in 1938 and later moved through Hong Kong, Singapore, Burma, and Rangoon before returning to Taiwan via Vietnam after Japan's defeat. Ian Apai, an Indigenous Truku woman from Hualien, was recruited for sewing work near a Japanese army post and was instead taken to a cave used as a comfort station, where she was forced to serve soldiers daily until 1946. Hwang Ah-tao was deceived at twenty by a "nurses wanted" advertisement and taken to a comfort station beside an air base in the mountains of Balikpapan, Indonesia.

Maria Rosa Luna Henson (1927-1997) of Luzon, Philippines, was the first Filipina survivor to testify publicly, doing so in September 1992. She was raped by Japanese soldiers at age fourteen, before she had begun menstruating, and later joined anti-Japanese guerrilla activity; in 1943 she was captured and held for nine months of confinement and repeated rape. Other Filipina survivors include Maxima Regala de la Cruz, abducted with her mother near San Ildefonso in Bulacan province, and Tomasa Dioso Salinog, whose father was killed by a Japanese soldier who then abducted her at age thirteen and confined her at a military post until Japan's defeat.

Jan Ruff-O'Herne (born 1923), a Dutch woman who had initially traveled to Indonesia to become a nun, was held at the Ambarawa internment camp after Japan's 1942 invasion of Java and was later confined for two months in a comfort station called "Nanamitei" in Semarang in 1944. She became the first Dutch survivor to testify publicly, in 1992, and later testified about her experiences before the U.S. House of Representatives.

Sirota Suzuko (a pseudonym, 1921-1993) was the first Japanese survivor to testify publicly. Sold by her impoverished family at age seventeen, she was taken together with Korean and Okinawan women to comfort stations across the South Pacific. After the war she returned to Japan but struggled with a difficult life and eventually entered a shelter; a memorial to her and other survivors was built in 1986 after her account became publicly known.

Rosalind Saw (born 1916, Penang, Malaysia) was confined in an army comfort station known as the "Donrotku Hotel" in Penang until early 1945 and gave birth to a Japanese soldier's child that February; she disclosed her experience publicly in 1994. Mardiyem (1929-2007) of Yogyakarta, Indonesia, was deceived at age thirteen by a local Japanese-appointed official and forced into a comfort station in Banjarmasin, where she became pregnant and was subjected to a forced abortion before being confined there until Japan's defeat; she later became an important figure in a survivors' network. Esmeralda Boe of East Timor was taken while still too young to have begun menstruating and was forced to serve three Japanese commanders over three years, including being made to sing for their entertainment.

In August 1993, after evidence of the Japanese military's involvement in the comfort station system became public, Chief Cabinet Secretary Kono Yohei issued the Kono Statement, Japan's first official acknowledgment that the military was directly or indirectly involved in establishing and managing comfort stations, and that many women were gathered against their own will through coercion or deception. The Kono Statement remains the Japanese government's official position on the issue, although conservative politicians in Japan have repeatedly called for it to be revised or retracted.

Starting in December 1991, Kim Hak-soon and other survivors filed lawsuits against the Japanese government, and survivors and their supporters from Korea, the Philippines, Korean residents of Japan, the Netherlands, China, Taiwan, and Hainan Island pursued roughly nine separate court cases in Japan. Japanese courts largely dismissed these suits on statute-of-limitations and pre-war state-immunity grounds. The one partial victory, a 1998 Shimonoseki district court ruling that recognized state responsibility for legislative inaction, was later overturned on appeal. Most cases did, however, result in Japanese courts formally acknowledging the historical facts of the victims' suffering as established evidence, even while denying legal remedies.

Coverage of the comfort women system in Japanese history textbooks expanded after the Kono Statement, appearing in all seven major Japanese high school textbook publishers by 1997 and in all middle school history textbooks that same year. A backlash led by the "Japanese Society for History Textbook Reform," founded in 1996, campaigned to remove such references; by 2006, references to comfort women had disappeared from every major Japanese middle school history textbook then in use.

In July 1995 Japan established the Asian Women's Fund, formally the "Asian Peace National Fund for Women," which used privately raised donations to pay "atonement money" to individual survivors together with a letter of apology from the prime minister, while the Japanese government separately funded medical and welfare support; the fund dissolved in March 2007. It reached 285 survivors in Korea, the Philippines, and Taiwan, 79 survivors in the Netherlands, and funded 69 elderly-care welfare facilities in Indonesia. Critics, including most Korean survivor-support organizations, argued that the fund substituted private donations for formal state compensation, reached only a fraction of victims, limited the prime minister's apology letter to only those who accepted the fund's payment, and did not resolve survivors' underlying demand for legal responsibility and apology from the Japanese state.

The comfort women issue was raised at the United Nations Human Rights Commission starting in 1992. UN Special Rapporteur Radhika Coomaraswamy investigated and submitted a report in 1996, and Special Rapporteur Gay McDougall submitted a further report in 1998; both concluded that the wartime comfort station system violated international law and that Japan bore responsibility for compensating victims and prosecuting those responsible. Beginning in 1995, the International Labour Organization's Committee of Experts likewise found in multiple reports that the comfort station system violated the Forced Labour Convention.

In July 2007, the United States House of Representatives passed House Resolution 121, led by Congressman Mike Honda, calling on Japan to formally and unambiguously apologize for and accept historical responsibility for the comfort women system; survivors testified at a February 2007 congressional hearing that helped build support for the resolution despite lobbying against it by the Japanese embassy and a newspaper advertisement placed by conservative Japanese lawmakers. Similar resolutions followed in the parliaments of the Netherlands, Canada, the European Parliament representing the 27 EU member states, the Philippines, Taiwan, and South Korea, as well as in several Japanese city assemblies.

In December 2000, women's organizations from across Asia convened the unofficial Women's International War Crimes Tribunal in Tokyo, intended to address the exclusion of wartime sexual violence from the postwar International Military Tribunal for the Far East (the Tokyo Trials, 1946-1948). Sixty-four survivors from eight countries participated as witnesses, and roughly 5,000 observers from 30 countries attended, including a joint North and South Korean team of prosecutors made possible by the political thaw following the 2000 inter-Korean summit. On December 12, 2000, the tribunal issued a summary judgment finding Japan's wartime Emperor Hirohito and other military and government officials guilty of crimes against humanity; a final judgment followed in The Hague in December 2001. The tribunal had no legal enforcement power, but was widely seen as a significant civil-society effort to establish moral and historical accountability, and its proceedings allowed some former Japanese soldiers to publicly confess their actions.

In 2001, Japan's public broadcaster NHK aired a documentary about the 2000 Women's Tribunal that was later found to have been altered under political pressure from ruling-party politicians seeking to minimize the comfort women issue; investigative reporting in 2005 revealed that Shinzo Abe, then deputy chief cabinet secretary, was among the officials involved in pressuring the broadcaster. A Japanese civic group, VAWW-NET Japan, sued NHK over the alteration; it won at the Tokyo High Court in 2007, but the ruling was overturned by Japan's Supreme Court in 2008.

When the comfort women issue emerged in Korea in January 1992, the South Korean government formed a task force under the Ministry of Foreign Affairs to investigate the historical record and register victims, publishing an interim report that July; a more thorough government investigation did not resume until 2004, under the Truth Commission on Forced Mobilization under Japanese Imperialism. In 1993, President Kim Young-sam announced that Korea would not seek material compensation from Japan in order to occupy the moral high ground, a policy that has remained largely in place since, even as the Korean government has separately provided survivors with domestic financial, medical, and housing support. Critics argue that this "no compensation demand" stance, while intended to keep moral and diplomatic pressure on Japan, has also constrained recognition of survivors' individual legal claims. In 2011, South Korea's Constitutional Court ruled that the government's failure to actively pursue a resolution of survivors' compensation claims through diplomatic channels with Japan was itself unconstitutional.

On January 8, 2021, the Seoul Central District Court ordered the Japanese government to pay 100 million won (roughly 90,000 US dollars at the time) to each of twelve Korean survivors and bereaved families who had sued for damages in the case registered as 2016 Gahap 505092. The court rejected Japan's argument that it was immune from being sued in a foreign country's courts, reasoning that the doctrine of sovereign immunity should not shield acts amounting to crimes against humanity. Japan did not participate in the proceedings and did not recognize the ruling, and it remains unenforced against the Japanese state; the case was nonetheless significant as the first ruling by a domestic court anywhere to hold the Japanese government directly liable to individual comfort women survivors.

A diary kept by a Korean man who worked as a paymaster and manager at Japanese military comfort stations in Burma and Singapore during 1943 and 1944 was discovered in a private museum in Gyeonggi-do, South Korea, and was translated and published by researchers in Seoul and Japan starting in 2013. The diary is considered a rare surviving administrative record of the day-to-day operation of comfort stations from the perspective of station management rather than the victims, and researchers have used it to help verify details about how the comfort station system was organized and run.

The House of Sharing's Museum of Japanese Military Sexual Slavery by Japan was made possible by contributions from many individuals, Buddhist and Catholic religious communities, civic organizations, universities, and companies in Korea and Japan, reflecting broad public support across both countries for preserving survivors' history and testimony.

The three most notable 'comfort women' survivor-activists and artists are Kim Hak-soon (1924-1997), whose public testimony on August 14, 1991 was the first by a Korean survivor and launched the international movement for awareness and redress; Kang Duk-kyung (1929-1997), who became known for powerfully expressing her experiences through painting; and Kim Sun-deok (1921-2004), who painted "The Flower That Could Not Blossom" (못다 핀 꽃), an image that became a symbol of the survivors' experience, and who was an energetic, active leader at the House of Sharing. Other remembered voices include Park Du-ri (1924-2006), known at the House of Sharing for expressing her pain and loneliness through humor and song; Park Ok-ryeon (1919-2011), respected as a steady, dependable leader among the survivors there; Kang Il-chul (1928-2026), who gave extensive oral testimony to researchers; and Park Ok-sun, who testified in Japan and rejected the term 'comfort women.' Among survivors from other countries, the first to testify publicly include Maria Rosa Luna Henson of the Philippines (1992), Jan Ruff-O'Herne of the Netherlands (1992), Wan Ai-hua of China, and Sirota Suzuko of Japan.

In Korea, the survivors of the Japanese military 'comfort women' system are affectionately and respectfully called "halmoni" (할머니), a Korean word meaning "grandmother." The term acknowledges their age and honors their experiences, and is used throughout the House of Sharing and its museum.

The House of Sharing was established in 1992 as a home for surviving victims of Japanese military sexual slavery. After Kim Hak-soon's groundbreaking 1991 testimony, many other survivors came forward. When the public learned that survivor Kang Duk-kyung was living in poor conditions, the Subcommittee on Women of the Korean Buddhist Commission on Human Rights launched a committee on August 1, 1992 to build a safe home, funded by donations. The House of Sharing first opened in Seogyo-dong, Seoul on October 3, 1992, housing five halmonies. The Buddhist monks quickly realized that some residents could not read or write Korean, so they began offering Korean language classes.

The House of Sharing did not stay in Seogyo-dong for long: complaints from neighbors forced it to relocate to Hyehwa-dong, Seoul, which sparked public outcry. In response, citizens and a local company donated land and resources to build a permanent residence in Gwangju, Gyeonggi Province. A groundbreaking ceremony took place on August 14, 1995, and the House of Sharing moved to its present location in Gwangju on December 9, 1995. It was incorporated as a social welfare organization by the Jogye Order of Korean Buddhism on November 14, 1996. Recognizing the importance of preserving the women's stories, the head Buddhist monk proposed building a museum, and the Museum of Sexual Slavery by the Japanese Military opened at the House of Sharing on August 14, 1998.

The House of Sharing serves three main purposes: to record the past, to educate the public about the history of the Japanese military 'comfort women', and to honor the victims who have passed away. The museum presents testimonies, historical documents, and personal artifacts, and invites visitors to reflect on the unresolved pain and injustice the women faced. It is a space of remembrance, respect, and healing. QR codes throughout the museum link to audio guides for each exhibit.

The House of Sharing started art classes for the victims in 1993 with the help of volunteering art teachers, beginning with artist Lee Kyun-shin. Starting with simple lines and figures, the halmonies learned to draw their surroundings and daily lives, and slowly began to recreate the painful memories of their past. The classes grew into art therapy sessions that became a powerful outlet for healing, reflection, and protest: through their paintings the victims condemned the crimes of Japan and worked to heal their own wounds. Many of these artworks are now considered iconic, and a selection of the paintings is displayed in the second museum building up the hill at the House of Sharing.

At both the entrance and exit of the House of Sharing museum stands the bronze sculpture "Who Could Ever...", which symbolizes the immense suffering endured by the women forced into sexual slavery. On the outer wall, the sculpture shows a young woman standing beneath auspicious clouds, bathed in the warm glow of morning sunlight, wearing a jokduri, a traditional Korean bridal crown. When asked, "If you were born again, what would you want to do?", many halmonies answered: "I want to get married and live in a happy family." The sculpture reflects that simple yet powerful wish — a life of joy and dignity denied to them in this world.

Outside the House of Sharing museum is a time capsule created on March 1, 2012 by the halmonies who lived there at the time. It is intended to remain sealed and will only be opened when the Japanese government issues an official apology and formally acknowledges that the 'comfort women' were forcibly recruited and exploited by the Japanese military.

The Japanese military called the women "military 'comfort women'" or "prostitutes" — terminology that distorts their actual experience, reflects only the military's perspective, and falsely implies consent and voluntary participation. Internationally accepted expressions such as "sexual slaves" and "victims of sexual violence" better reveal what the women actually endured. Despite this, the term 'comfort women' remains widely used, both because it is more commonly recognized and because some survivors have said they do not wish to be referred to as 'sex slaves.'

Imperial Japan invaded Manchuria in 1931 and began its long war with China. As sexually transmitted diseases spread among Japanese soldiers due to rape crimes the military could not control, the Imperial Japanese government created the military 'comfort women' system, placing soldiers and 'comfort women' under military supervision and control. The first comfort station was set up by the Japanese Navy in Shanghai in 1932, and from late 1937 — after the outbreak of full-scale war with China and the Rape of Nanking, which sharply increased demand — the Japanese military took a systematic, active role in establishing comfort stations.

Imperial Japan forcibly mobilized women as military 'comfort women' from Japan itself, colonial Joseon (Korea), occupied China, the Philippines, Indonesia, Papua New Guinea, and other territories. Most of the women were teenagers. Many were deceived by recruiters' promises such as "I will find you a job at a factory" or "I will help you earn a lot of money"; others were kidnapped, at times with the involvement of town officials, policemen, and military personnel, or abducted and sold by comfort station owners and recruiters — behind whom stood units of the Japanese military. The women were transported to comfort stations by military trucks, trains, and ships.

Life in the comfort stations was governed by military regulations covering hours of use, fees, venereal disease, holidays, and sanitation. A station could hold anywhere from a few women to as many as 60 or 70. The women's availability was tracked on schedule charts recording their assigned names, ages — some as young as sixteen — and whether they were suffering from sexually transmitted diseases or menstruating. Their real names were deliberately not recorded, which could have allowed them to seek justice later or serve as evidence against the military; this anonymity further erased their identities. Women who resisted or broke the rules were beaten or otherwise punished. When a unit was dispatched to an area without comfort stations, women were forced to serve the entire troop.

The services demanded of the women were scheduled by military rank: privates typically visited in the early morning, petty officers in the early evening, and high-ranking officers were allowed to stay overnight. Fees differed according to the hours of use and the ethnicity of the women, and soldiers paid with military-issued scrip or comfort station tickets — but the money went to the station owners, not the women. A few stations were run on a no-fee basis. Kept in constant poverty and entirely dependent on the station for food and shelter, the women were economically trapped in the system, unable to escape.

Comfort stations took different forms depending on whether the military unit was stationary or mobile. Stationary comfort stations were set up in permanent buildings — newly built by the military or renovated from residents' homes — with tiny, panel-walled rooms; in tropical areas such as Palau they were built with palm leaves. Mobile comfort stations accompanied troops on the move, set up in temporary locations such as military barracks, sentry boxes, trenches, or military trucks. Stations displayed signs such as "00 Comfort Station," and civilian managers posted propaganda slogans in Japanese such as "Japanese ladies serve you with their body and heart" or "Welcome, victorious warriors of the sacred war" to normalize the exploitation inside.

In terms of management there were two main types of comfort stations. Military-designated comfort stations were managed by civilians — brothel owners or contractors, and in Southeast Asia business organizations of local Japanese civilians used as fronts — while the military retained supervisory authority. Military-run comfort stations were fully controlled by the military itself, including supervision, management, logistics, and administration; stations run by civilian employees of the military belonged to this category, and many were set up at the beginning of the Sino-Japanese War and on the front lines.

The drug Salvarsan, commonly known as "No. 606," was a toxic, yellowish powder used as a treatment for syphilis. It was administered to 'comfort women' during mandatory, regularly scheduled medical examinations, ostensibly to prevent and treat venereal disease, but also to deter pregnancy, induce abortions, and even sterilize the women. The injections often contained harmful substances such as mercury, causing severe side effects, long-term health damage, and in many cases death. What was framed as medical care was in reality part of a system of control that prioritized military needs over the victims' lives. A sample of the drug is displayed at the House of Sharing museum.

The House of Sharing museum includes a replica of a comfort station room. Outside each room in a real station hung wooden panels bearing the Japanese names forcibly assigned to the women — their real names and identities stripped away — and in some stations the panels were flipped to show whether the room was occupied. Despite the cramped space, a woman was forced to eat, sleep, and wash in the same room, living under constant surveillance with little to no freedom. A washbasin in the corner was reportedly used to clean themselves and the condoms between forced encounters, but the basins were rarely sanitized. The "satku," a Japanese military-issued condom, was frequently reused among multiple soldiers, dramatically increasing the risk of infection; one is displayed in the museum.

Newspaper advertisements recruiting Japanese military 'comfort women' are displayed at the House of Sharing museum. Because the term 'comfort women' was created by the Japanese military, most people who saw such advertisements did not understand what the role actually entailed, and those who responded were deceived or coerced; recruiters also used the ads as cover for kidnapping and trafficking. Today the Japanese government points to such advertisements as "evidence" that the women volunteered willingly. This interpretation is misleading: the women were never informed of the conditions they would face, and the military and government actively participated in recruitment — the advertisements themselves serve as evidence of official involvement.

During the Battle of Okinawa, the Japanese military built at least 150 military sexual slavery facilities on Okinawa, constructing its own buildings or renovating public facilities and residences wherever soldiers camped. Women of Okinawa were abused in these facilities: women from Ceji, one of Okinawa's largest quarters, who could not overcome poverty, debts, and high interest rates, initially resisted conscription, but after Ceji was destroyed in a sudden attack in October 1944, most were forced into sexual slavery. Many Korean women were also enslaved on Okinawa, either moved there with the Japanese military from comfort stations in China or newly taken from Korea. Okinawan residents saw these women come to town for food or escape through caves, and after the war many lived in refugee camps. According to U.S. military documents, around 150 Korean women returned home after Japan's defeat, but many others could not.

The Battle of Okinawa was the only battle of the Pacific War fought on Japanese soil that directly engaged civilians. Japanese soldiers set Okinawa as the last defensive line in the name of national defense and 'protection of the Emperor.' Fighting between the U.S. and Japanese militaries continued for over three months from late March 1945; Japanese soldiers slaughtered civilians or forced them to commit suicide, and over 150,000 people — approximately a quarter of Okinawa's population — were killed. Okinawa, formerly the independent Ryukyu Kingdom with its own culture and trade until the Meiji government annexed it in 1879, was occupied and governed by the U.S. military until 1972; around 75% of the U.S. military bases in Japan remain on Okinawa today.

Bae Bong-gi (1914-1991), recognized as the first unofficial testifier, was born in Yesan, Chungcheongnam-do in 1914, the daughter of a poor servant family. Deceived by a recruiter who promised her "a job at a place like paradise," she arrived in Okinawa in November 1944 and was forced into sexual slavery. After Japan's defeat she was detained at a civilian prisoner-of-war camp and missed the homebound ship, remaining in Okinawa as an illegal resident. When Okinawa was returned to Japan in 1972, she was forced to reveal her past as a Japanese military 'comfort woman' in order to avoid deportation — 14 years before Kim Hak-soon's public testimony. Three years later she obtained an extension of stay as a registered alien. Her story reached the media in 1977, making her the first victim of Japanese military sexual slavery known to the world, though she lived much of her life behind emotional walls. She never made it back to Korea, and passed away in Okinawa in October 1991, shortly after her birthday, at the age of 77. Her testimony laid the groundwork for future survivors to come forward.

Kim Hak-soon decided to come forward after the Japanese government in 1990 denied the existence of the 'comfort women' system. In her own words: "I made a resolution after seeing what was in the newspapers and on the news. Things are not right. I need to set the record straight. They are lying and I cannot understand why they would lie... We must record these things that were forced upon us. The same atrocities must not be repeated in the future." She gave her testimony at a press conference on August 14, 1991 — a date now commemorated as the International Memorial Day for 'Comfort Women' — and on December 6, 1991 she and two other victims, together with former soldiers and civilians attached to the Japanese military, filed a lawsuit against the Japanese government demanding an official apology and compensation. Her courage became a catalyst for hundreds of women in Korea, China, the Philippines, Indonesia, and the Netherlands to share their own stories.

At the House of Sharing museum, portraits of known 'comfort women' victims are displayed alongside several blacked-out portraits. The blacked-out portraits symbolize the many victims who were never able to come forward — some out of fear of retaliation against their families, some due to concerns about being ostracized by their communities, and many because of the deep shame and trauma caused by their experiences. Also displayed is the well-known photograph of Park Young-shim, a pregnant North Korean prisoner of war who survived the comfort station system and later returned to visit the site where she had been held.

The Wednesday Demonstrations, organized by the Korean Council for the Women Drafted for Military Sexual Slavery by Japan (also known as Chong Dae Hyup), began at noon on January 8, 1992 in front of the Japanese Embassy in Seoul, coinciding with a visit by then Japanese Prime Minister Kiichi Miyazawa. Victims living at the House of Sharing regularly participated, and the weekly protests have continued rain or shine, joined by the general public and domestic and international organizations, including civic groups from Japan and Southeast Asia. The 1,000th weekly demonstration was held on December 14, 2011, and the 1,210th on December 30, 2015.

The Wednesday Demonstrations will continue until the Japanese government meets seven key demands: (1) acknowledgment of the war crime; (2) full disclosure of the truth about military sexual slavery; (3) an official apology from the Japanese government; (4) legal reparations to the victims; (5) punishment of those responsible for the war crime; (6) accurate representation of the crime in history textbooks; and (7) the erection of a memorial for the victims and the establishment of a historical museum. Only when these goals are fulfilled, the organizers say, can the surviving halmonies find true peace and healing.

The movement to expose the 'comfort women' issue took shape between 1988 and 1991. In February 1988, three members of Korea Church Women United, including professor Yoon Jung-ok, traced the footsteps of the 'comfort women' from Fukuoka to Okinawa. In January 1990, Yoon Jung-ok's four-part series "Jungsindae: Traces of Vindictive Spirits" was published in the South Korean newspaper Hankyoreh. In June 1990, Japanese Diet member Motooka Shoji asked the Japanese government to investigate the issue, but a Ministry of Labor official replied that the 'comfort women' "were in the hands of private entrepreneurs" and refused any investigation. On November 16, 1990, thirty-seven organizations and individuals formed the Korean Council for the Women Drafted for Military Sexual Slavery by Japan (the Korean Council).

Key events of 1992-1993: On January 11, 1992, the Japanese newspaper Asahi reported that Professor Yoshimi Yoshiaki of Chuo University had found records in the library of Japan's Defense Ministry research institute proving the Japanese military's establishment of comfort stations and recruitment of 'comfort women.' On January 17, 1992, Prime Minister Kiichi Miyazawa declared his remorse and tendered an apology to the people of the Republic of Korea in a speech at the South Korean National Assembly. On February 25, 1992, the Korean government opened a victim report center and began receiving reports and testimonies. On December 25, 1992, ten Korean women — victims of sexual slavery and former labor corps members — filed the "Gwanbu Lawsuit" (also called the Shimonoseki Lawsuit) in the Shimonoseki branch of Japan's Yamaguchi district court. In April 1993, Song Shin-do, a Korean victim living in Japan, filed a lawsuit in the Tokyo district court. On August 4, 1993, the Kono Statement was issued.

Key events of 1994-1999: In August 1994, Japanese Prime Minister Tomiichi Murayama announced plans for a private consolation fund, and the Asian Women's Fund was founded on July 19, 1995, with 43 domestic and international groups, including the Korean Council, immediately declaring their opposition. In September 1995, the UN Fourth World Conference on Women in Beijing passed recommendations on prosecuting war criminals of sexual slavery and demanding full compensation from Japan. In December 1995, Murayama stated that Japan could not compensate victims individually and claimed the wartime policy "was not against international law at that time." The Coomaraswamy report was published in 1996 and the McDougall report in 1998, and the ILO expert committee concluded in multiple reports from 1995 onward that the system violated the Forced Labour Convention, urging action given the victims' advanced age. On April 27, 1998, the Shimonoseki branch court ruled that Japan should compensate three Korean victims 300,000 yen each — the first ruling to acknowledge state responsibility. In August 1999, the UN Sub-Commission adopted a resolution that rights and obligations under international law regarding such violations "could not be extinguished by peace treaty or amnesty," and the California State Assembly adopted a resolution by Assemblyman Mike Honda urging Japan to formally apologize and pay reparations.

Key events of 2000-2008: On September 18, 2000, victims from Korea, China, Taiwan, and the Philippines sued the Japanese government in the U.S. District Court in Washington, D.C.; after five and a half years the suit was dismissed for lack of jurisdiction under the sovereign immunity doctrine and the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act. The Women's International War Crimes Tribunal was held in Tokyo in December 2000. In 2001, the Hiroshima High Court reversed the Shimonoseki ruling, and in 2003 the Supreme Court of Japan finalized its dismissal on the ground of "extinctive prescription." In 2003, the UN CEDAW committee called on Japan to find a lasting solution. In 2004, V-Day and Amnesty International adopted the 'comfort women' issue as a campaign focus. In July 2006, 64 victims filed a constitutional complaint in Korea's Constitutional Court over the government's failure to take diplomatic action. In July 2007, the U.S. House of Representatives passed House Resolution 121. In October 2008, the Korean National Assembly adopted a resolution urging Japan to officially apologize and compensate the victims.

Key events of 2011-2015: On August 30, 2011, the Constitutional Court of Korea ruled that the Korean government's failure to seek a solution with Japan on compensation infringed the victims' basic human rights and violated the Constitution. In October 2011, South Korea raised the issue at the UN General Assembly. In August 2012, Japanese Prime Minister Yoshihiko Noda claimed "there was no evidence that the 'comfort women' were abducted or taken by force." In 2013, the Shimane Prefectural Assembly demanded the Abe Cabinet correct its denials, while Chief Cabinet Secretary Suga objected to 'comfort women' memorial statues abroad. In January 2014, the U.S. House passed its first bill urging follow-through on the 2007 resolution, and in July 2014 survivors Kang Il-chul and Lee Ok-sun met with White House and State Department officials. In 2014, former Prime Minister Murayama publicly acknowledged Japan's "indescribable wrongdoings." In February 2015, Prime Minister Abe called the issue "not a political or diplomatic problem"; in March 2015, the U.S. State Department reaffirmed the definition of the system as trafficking and a terrible violation of human rights; in May 2015, 187 international historians urged Japan to officially recognize and apologize; and in August 2015, the Heilongjiang local government in China revealed wartime documents showing the Japanese military had deceived and forced 2,000 Korean women into sexual slavery.

In December 2015, the Korean and Japanese governments announced an agreement on the 'comfort women' issue at a joint press conference of the two countries' foreign ministers. The victims vehemently opposed the agreement because the Japanese government did not clearly acknowledge its national and legal responsibilities. In March 2016, 29 victims and 41 family members filed a constitutional appeal claiming the agreement violated their basic rights; in December 2019 the Constitutional Court dismissed the complaint, ruling the agreement was not subject to constitutional review. UN bodies also criticized the agreement: in 2018 the Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination found it was not a victim-centered approach, and the Committee on Enforced Disappearances criticized Japan's claim that the issue had been "finally and irreversibly resolved."

Key events of 2016-2024: In March 2016, the UN CEDAW committee recommended that Japan stop minimizing its responsibility and include the issue in textbooks. In April 2016, the San Francisco Board of Education adopted a resolution to include 'comfort women' content in history curricula. In May 2016, an international solidarity committee of 14 civic groups from 8 countries applied to register 2,744 'comfort women'-related materials as a UNESCO Memory of the World. In August 2016, Seoul unveiled a memorial site for the victims in Yejang-dong. In March 2017, the first Statue of Peace in Europe was unveiled in Wiesent, Bavaria, Germany, followed by one in Frankfurt in March 2020. In November 2017, the UN Human Rights Council's Universal Periodic Review recommended that Japan sincerely apologize and compensate the victims, and on November 24, 2017, the Korean National Assembly passed the law designating August 14 as the official Day of Remembrance for the victims; the first government-sponsored ceremony was held on August 14, 2018 at the National Memorial Garden in Cheonan. On March 1, 2019, survivor and activist Lee Yong-su attended the 100th anniversary ceremony of the March 1st Movement as one of 33 national representatives. On January 8, 2021, twelve plaintiffs, including the late Bae Chun-hee, won their damages lawsuit against Japan in the Seoul Central District Court. On March 1, 2024, the National Catholic Action held a mass in front of the former Japanese Embassy calling for Japan's official apology and legal compensation.

The Korean government supports the surviving victims in several ways: living wages under the National Basic Living Security Act, medical support under the Medical Care Assistance Act, state aid for livelihood and stability, and preferential housing leases for those who do not own a home. The state and local governments can also carry out memorial projects — collection of historical data, research, archiving, preservation, exhibitions, education, and outreach — and subsidize such projects when local governments, organizations, or individuals perform them. Starting in 2014, the Korean and Japanese governments held director-general-level meetings on the issue, which led to the December 2015 agreement that victims strongly opposed.

The background of the Kono Statement: in January 1992, after Professor Yoshimi Yoshiaki's documentary evidence was revealed, the Japanese government admitted coercion in the 'comfort women' system, and Prime Minister Miyazawa expressed regret and promised an investigation. The first investigation, released in July 1992, was criticized for its lack of content, so a second investigation was conducted, resulting in the Kono Statement of August 4, 1993. The statement acknowledged that the Japanese military was directly or indirectly involved in the establishment and management of the comfort stations and the transfer of 'comfort women'; that recruitment was conducted mainly by private recruiters acting at the military's request; that in many cases women were recruited against their own will through coaxing and coercion, with administrative and military personnel at times directly taking part; and that the women lived in misery at the stations under a coercive atmosphere.

Following the Kono Statement, all Japanese high school history textbooks from 1995 and all middle school history textbooks from 1997 included sections about the 'comfort women.' However, Japanese radical conservatives began attempts to amend the statement from 2001. In 2014, the Shinzo Abe cabinet attempted to revise the Kono Statement under the pretext of reviewing it, and in 2015 established a 'Review Committee for War and History,' continuing attempts to distort the modern history of the 'comfort women.' In March 2007, Abe had claimed "there was no evidence to prove there was coercion as initially suggested" — a statement that defied the widely accepted historical record. Denial by revisionist historians and some major newspapers, along with the eradication of 'comfort women' content from textbooks, has fostered widespread indifference in Japanese society.

Critics identified five main problems with the Asian Women's Fund (the "National Fund"): (1) it was not compensation from the state, which the victims demanded; (2) it did not reach all victims; (3) the prime minister's apology letter was addressed only to victims who accepted the consolation money; (4) even recipients could not recover their dignity, as the fund divided victims and support organizations; and (5) the Japanese government remained indifferent to genuinely solving the problem. The 'comfort women' resolutions passed in many countries in 2007 highlighted that the fund did not meet the victims' demands. Separately, after the 1998 Shimonoseki ruling recognized that compensation legislation was needed, Japanese opposition parties repeatedly submitted bills from April 2000 for an official apology and national reparations, but the ruling Liberal Democratic Party kept the bills from even reaching a hearing.

Japanese courts dismissed the survivors' lawsuits mainly on three legal grounds: the "exclusion period" (a principle that claims to damages are extinguished after a certain time), the "no response policy" (a principle that the current government bears no responsibility for actions of the prewar government under the old constitution), and only once — in the 1998 Shimonoseki ruling, later overturned — was "legislative nonfeasance" (the state's failure to enact needed legislation) accepted as a basis for compensation. Despite the dismissals, most rulings formally acknowledged the historical facts of the victims' suffering.

The Women's International War Crimes Tribunal of 2000 was organized because the international community had failed to produce meaningful remedies. Its purposes were to provide the victims' perspective, which had been excluded from the International Military Tribunal for the Far East (the Tokyo Tribunal, 1946-1948); to collect evidence of the crimes; and to confirm the responsibility of the Japanese government and individual perpetrators. On December 12, 2000, the tribunal pronounced former Emperor Hirohito guilty of crimes against humanity and held the Japanese government responsible for its war crimes; the final judgment on December 4, 2001 in The Hague found all ten accused, including Hirohito, guilty. Though not legally binding, the tribunal publicized to the world that the Japanese military had knowingly committed war crimes.

In June 2014, a group of victims filed civil and criminal complaints against Professor Park Yu-ha for defaming them in her book "Comfort Women of the Empire," which argued that Japan did not forcibly recruit 'comfort women' and portrayed them as prostitutes who collaborated voluntarily. In February 2015, the Seoul Eastern District Court sided with the victims, imposing an injunction banning the book's publication and sale, and in November 2015 South Korean prosecutors indicted Park for defamation. Separately, in June 2015, two South Korean survivors filed a lawsuit in the U.S. District Court in San Francisco against former Emperor Hirohito, former Prime Minister Nobusuke Kishi, and seven Japanese corporations, including Mitsubishi and Sankei Shimbun, for crimes against humanity and defamation, demanding $20 million.

U.S. House Resolution 121, passed on July 30, 2007, resolved that the Government of Japan should: (1) formally acknowledge, apologize, and accept historical responsibility in a clear and unequivocal manner for its Imperial Armed Forces' coercion of young women into sexual slavery; (2) have the Prime Minister of Japan deliver the apology as a public statement in his official capacity; (3) clearly and publicly refute any claims that the sexual enslavement and trafficking of the 'comfort women' never occurred; and (4) educate current and future generations about this horrible crime. Survivors testified at the first public hearing in the House in February 2007, while the Japanese embassy lobbied against the resolution and conservative Japanese lawmakers published an opposing advertisement called 'THE FACT' in the Washington Post.

Moon Pil-gi (1925-2008) was born in Jinyang, Gyeongsangnam-do. In 1943, at 18 years old, she followed a neighbor who deceived her with the promise of education and delivered her to a comfort station in China. Unable to recover from the trauma even after the war ended, she roamed until eventually settling at the House of Sharing, where she was known as a quiet and peaceful figure. Even while receiving treatment for kidney failure, she recorded a video message asking the U.S. House of Representatives to pass House Resolution 121 urging Japan's formal apology. Her health deteriorated soon after, and she passed away on March 5, 2008, at the age of 83.

Hwang Geum-ju (1922-2013) was born in Buyeo, Chungcheongnam-do. While employed as a domestic worker, she was forced to take the place of the daughter of the household she served; what was supposed to be a munitions factory turned out to be a comfort station in China under the jurisdiction of Japanese troops, named 'Hinomaru.' In 1992 she testified before the UN Human Rights Subcommittee in Geneva, Switzerland about Japan's crimes, and afterward traveled through the United States and Canada to spread the truth about what women like her had suffered. She passed away from an illness on January 3, 2013, at the age of 91.

Kim Gun-ja (1926-2017) was born in Pyeongchang, Gangwon-do, and was orphaned as a teenager. In 1942, at the age of 16, while running an errand for her adoptive father, she was captured and taken to China, where she suffered for four years as a victim of Japanese military sexual slavery. After her release she walked for three months to return to Cheorwon, Gangwon-do. In 2007 she testified before members of the U.S. Congress, stating that she had been forced to serve over 40 men in a day and that she still bore the knife wounds slashed onto her body. Despite her hardships she constantly gave to others, routinely helping students who were struggling financially. She remained outspoken about Japan's war crimes until her passing on July 23, 2017, at the age of 91.

Choi Seon-soon (1927-2013) was born in Gochang, Jeollabuk-do. In 1942, at 15 years old, while on an errand to buy medicine for her ailing father, she was trafficked to Japan, where she suffered as a victim of Japanese military sexual slavery and became severely addicted to the opium administered during her enslavement. After her release she returned to Korea, made her way to Gwangju, Jeollanam-do, and married a man who had himself been a victim of forced labor drafted by the Japanese military. Until her death on August 24, 2013, at the age of 86, she suffered from severe nightmares about Japanese soldiers.

Moon Myeong-geum (1917-2000) was born in Gwangyang, Jeollanam-do. At 17, while visiting Hadong, she was captured and trafficked into China, where she suffered for ten years as a victim of Japanese military sexual slavery. Much of her later life is unaccounted for; she was found living in a nursing home not far from the place of her captivity. In 1999, the eight victims living at the House of Sharing organized a teahouse fundraiser to bring her back to South Korea after 64 years away. Known for her warm heart and deep sense of responsibility for others, she donated her life savings of $40,000 to the victims of the Vietnam War. She passed away on November 3, 2000, at the age of 83.

Lee Yong-nyeo (1926-2013) was born in Yeoju, Gyeonggi-do. At 16, she left her job as a domestic worker after hearing about a money-making opportunity from the woman of the household she served, and was trafficked to Taiwan, Singapore, and finally Myanmar. After her release from the comfort station she was sent to a prisoner-of-war camp in Myanmar before finding her way back to Busan, Korea. She suffered from post-traumatic stress disorder and struggled to settle in one place, moving between her son's home and the House of Sharing. In 2000 she testified at the Women's International War Crimes Tribunal in Tokyo. She passed away on August 11, 2013 — a few days before Korea's Independence Day — without ever receiving a formal apology from the Japanese government. She was 87 years old.

Kim Ok-ju (1923-2000) was born in Daegu, Gyeongsangbuk-do. From the age of 11 she worked as a domestic worker for a Japanese household; at 16, urged by the owner of the house, Masumoto, to take a paying job in Incheon, she was instead trafficked into China and suffered six years as a victim of sexual slavery. She strongly empathized with other victims of war and requested that her life savings of $20,000 be donated to victims of the Vietnam War. She enjoyed generous discussions on life and the world until her final days, and passed away on January 16, 2000, after a battle with liver cancer, at the age of 77.

Ji Dol-yi (1923-2008) was born in Gyeongju, Gyeongsangbuk-do. She married a Korean man at 18 and settled with him in Japan, returning alone to Korea when her husband was drafted into the Japanese military — his final words to her were, "Wait for me, I promise I'll come back alive for you." In 1945, on her way to what was promised to be a job at a textile factory, she was taken to a comfort station in China. Even after her release she could not find her way home, and settled alone in a village just two kilometers from the comfort station where she had been enslaved. News of her survival emerged in 1997, and in 2000 she was invited to live at the House of Sharing. Having trouble communicating in Korean and with difficulty hearing, she spent much of her time alone. She passed away on February 6, 2008, still waiting for her husband to return, at the age of 85.

Yoo Hee-nam (1929-2016) was born in Asan, Chungcheongnam-do. In 1943, at 14 years old, she was taken by Japanese military police to Shimonoseki, where she suffered as a victim of Japanese military sexual slavery; she later escaped with the help of a Japanese civilian while on a boat transporting her from Osaka to Singapore. For many years she suffered from insomnia and heart disease, and in 2009 was diagnosed with lung cancer. At the House of Sharing she worked endlessly to obtain a formal apology from the Japanese government, and as part of her activism sued the Japanese government and corporations for defamation and crimes against humanity. Yoo Hee-nam passed away from lung cancer on July 10, 2016 — this is the date of her death anniversary (기일). She was 87 years old.

Kim Hwa-sun (1926-2012) was born in Pyongyang, Pyeongannam-do, to a severely impoverished family. At 15, she followed a Japanese soldier on the promise of delicious food and was trafficked to a comfort station in Singapore. Though she returned to Korea after her release, she never made it back to her hometown, living alone in Daejeon before permanently settling at the House of Sharing in 2008. Though her life was marked by loneliness and hardship, she is remembered for her generosity: she donated funds to establish scholarships for students and to build wells in Cambodia, and willed her entire life savings of 6,000,000 won to the House of Sharing. She passed away on June 13, 2012, at the age of 86.

Bae Chun-hee (1923-2014) was born in Sangju, Gyeongsangbuk-do. At 18, after applying to what she believed was a paying job, she was forcefully taken along with her friend Bongsoon to China, where she suffered four years of sexual enslavement by the Japanese military. She spent more than 30 years afterward in China and Japan — thriving in Japan as a singer specializing in enka songs — before finally returning to Korea in 1981. A gifted artist and linguist fluent in Japanese, Chinese, Russian, and Korean, she continued developing her artistry at the House of Sharing until her passing on June 8, 2014, at the age of 91. She was among the twelve plaintiffs whose 2021 court victory against the Japanese government was decided after her death.

Moon Pil-gi, Hwang Geum-ju, Kim Gun-ja, Choi Seon-soon, Moon Myeong-geum, Lee Yong-nyeo, Kim Ok-ju, Ji Dol-yi, Yoo Hee-nam, Kim Hwa-sun, Bae Chun-hee, Bae Bong-gi, Park Du-ri, Park Ok-ryeon, Kim Sun-deok, Kang Duk-kyung, and Kang Il-chul are among the halmonies whose lives are commemorated at the House of Sharing, where their biographies, photographs, and in many cases artworks are displayed so that visitors can remember each woman as an individual, not only as a victim.

After Japan colonized Korea beginning in 1905, colonial Joseon was left impoverished: most of Korea's resources were extracted and redirected to support Japan's imperial expansion across Asia. This poverty made young Korean women especially vulnerable to recruiters — often employed by or working with the military — who tricked them with offers of seemingly legitimate, well-paying jobs in hospitals or factories; others were trafficked or abducted outright into the system of military sexual slavery.

At the beginning of the war, most 'comfort women' were taken from Japan's colonies, including Korea, China, and Taiwan, and were forced to accompany Japanese military units, moving from place to place as the army advanced. As the Japanese military expanded further into Southeast Asia during World War II, local women in those regions were also coerced or forced into the system. The House of Sharing museum displays photographs and testimonies of victims from North and South Korea, Japan, China, East Timor, Taiwan, the Philippines, and the Netherlands, along with two maps showing the locations of comfort stations in Korea and around the world.

The Kim Hak-soon exhibit at the House of Sharing museum includes a timeline of her life and, at the center of the survivors' portraits, a button that plays an audio recording of her testimony in Korean, with a transcript displayed in both English and Korean. Visitors are invited to listen closely and feel the anger, frustration, and sorrow in her voice. In her testimony she said: "They dragged me by the arm telling me to follow them... I resisted because I was scared, and then they kicked me telling me to go on... They kicked me and told me that I would be safe if I followed their orders, but I would die on the spot if I refused to listen to them... Please make me sixteen years old again — I want a fresh, new start. We must keep this in mind. We must record these things that were forced upon us. The same atrocities must not be repeated in the future."

At the end of the House of Sharing museum exhibition, visitors are welcome to take any of the books or items displayed on the table, and may direct further questions to the museum by email or to the museum's AI chatbot, Nanumi.

Additional legal and international milestones: In January 1994, victims of Japanese military sexual slavery together with former prisoners of war brought a lawsuit in the Tokyo district court. In June 1996, twenty-four members of Japan's National Diet, including Councilor Motooka Shoji, proposed a bill to establish an investigation commission on the issue of wartime sexual slavery victims. In June 2001, the ILO Committee on the Application of Standards voted unanimously to discuss the Japanese military 'comfort women' issue as an agenda item of the International Labour Conference, but aggressive lobbying by the Japanese government kept it off the final agenda. In August 2001, the first trial of the lawsuit brought by victims from South Korea, China, Taiwan, and the Philippines was held at the U.S. District Court in Washington, D.C. In October 2002, the Tokyo District Court dismissed the claims of nine Taiwanese women who had been forced to provide sex to Japanese soldiers during the war and had asked for a public apology and compensation.

The United Nations process developed in stages: after the issue was raised at the UN Human Rights Commission in 1992, the commission appointed Radhika Coomaraswamy as its special rapporteur on violence against women in April 1994; she conducted research throughout the Korean peninsula and Japan in 1995 and submitted her report in January 1996, which the commission agreed to "pay attention" to. Gay McDougall, special rapporteur of the UN Sub-Commission on the Promotion and Protection of Human Rights, submitted the "Special Report Related to Systematic Rape, Sexual Slavery, and Slavery-like Practices in Armed Conflict" in June 1998. In the ILO, the Federation of Korean Trade Unions (1995) and the Korean Confederation of Trade Unions (1998) expressed solidarity with the Korean Council and presented written opinions to the ILO expert committee, which concluded in November 1995 that the comfort station system violated the Forced Labour Convention and took up the issue again in its reports of 1997, 1999, and 2001-2004.

The 'comfort women' issue is not only a matter between Korea and Japan: it raises broader questions of war crimes, racism, and discrimination against women. These underlying aspects of the issue still present problems that need to be addressed within Korean society itself, and more attention to them is needed.

The Wednesday Demonstrations take place at noon in front of the Japanese Embassy in Seoul, in the Kwanhun-dong area. The participation of the victims themselves — including halmonies from the House of Sharing — has had a great impact on the demonstrations. As the protests gained momentum and drew growing attention to the issue, active joint activities developed between the victims and various organizations, and civic organizations from Japan and Southeast Asia joined, drawing international attention. The Wednesday Demonstrations have been the central force in pressing the victims' demands and will continue until Japan officially apologizes and pays individual reparations.

Periodic verbal abuse by Japanese governors and internet campaigns by revisionist historians have spread the misconceptions that the 'comfort women' system was "just a business," that the Japanese government bears no responsibility for it, or that it never existed at all — fueling widespread public indifference in Japanese society toward this history.

As an additional form of support, when a corporation or group related to the victims of Japanese military sexual slavery carries out commemorative or welfare services, the Korean state or local government can offer a free loan of national or public property.
